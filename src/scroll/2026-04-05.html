Our Vision & Mission

Our vision is to see lives transformed by the power of the gospel, rooted in God’s Word and the Reformed tradition, in order to create a culture of discipleship at FPC where disciples WORSHIP holistically, GROW relationally in Christ, and SERVE boldly in the world for the glory of God. Our mission is to accomplish this by “Being Disciples and Making Disciples.”

*The Profession of Faith Heidelberg Catechism, Q.45

Leader: How does Christ’s resurrection benefit us?

All: First, by his resurrection he has overcome death, so that he could make us share in the righteousness which he had obtained for us by his death. Second, by his power we too are raised up to a new life. Third, Christ’s resurrection is to us a sure pledge of our glorious resurrection.

Heidelberg Catechism The Heidelberg Catechism, written in 1563, has long been cherished in the Reformed church for its warm, pastoral expression of the gospel, framing the Christian life around our only comfort in life and in death. It was later affirmed at the Synod of Dort (1618) as a faithful summary of biblical truth.

Crown Him with Many Crowns The third verse beautifully reflects the 19th-century evangelical emphasis on Christ’s resurrection as the centerpiece of Christian hope. It highlights the wounds of Christ still visible in glory — capturing a deeply historic Christian theme: that the risen Christ is the same crucified Savior.

Thine Is the Glory The text was written in French by Swiss pastor Edmond Budry in the late 19th century (1884). It quickly spread across Europe as a powerful Resurrection hymn. Its repeated refrain — “Thine is the glory, risen, conquering Son” — captures the central proclamation of Easter: not just that Christ lives, but that He reigns in victory.

Sermon Outline

“The Resurrection Truth”

1 Corinthians 15:1–22 • Rev. Waring Porter

Most of the world knows that Christianity is centered around the resurrection of Jesus Christ. It is foundational to our weekly worship, and is specifically celebrated and remembered on Easter Sunday. God came to earth in human form, became a man, lived a perfect life, died on the cross so all of our sins could be forgiven, and He said, “I’m going to let these people kill Me and prove I am who I say I am, the Christ — God’s anointed one, by raising Myself back to life three days later.” Following His crucifixion, three days later He comes back to life. He’s walking around Jerusalem. Hundreds of people see Him. The most significant event in history has happened. If we are going to understand the gospel rightly, if it truly is of first importance, then we must understand what the resurrection means and why it matters. In these verses, Paul helps us see three foundational resurrection truths:

I.  The Certainty of the Resurrection (vv. 1–11)

II.  The Power of the Resurrection (vv. 12–15)

III.  The Hope of the Resurrection (vv. 16–19)

Resources: Calvin’s Commentaries (Vol. XVII), John Calvin; Matthew, William Hendrickson; Matthew Henry’s Commentary (Vols. 1, 6); The Case for Christ, Lee Strobel
